Hair Growth Cycle Understanding

Master the science of hair growth: anagen (growth), catagen (transition), and telogen (rest) phases to understand why hair sheds and how to optimize health.

Why Understanding the Hair Growth Cycle Matters

Every hair on your head goes through a continuous growth cycle with three distinct phases. Understanding this cycle is crucial for managing expectations about hair health, interpreting shedding patterns, and optimizing your routine. Many people panic when they see hair loss, not realizing that normal shedding is actually a sign of a healthy growth cycle.

At any given time, approximately 85-90% of your scalp hair is in the anagen (growth) phase, 1-3% is in the catagen (transition) phase, and 10-15% is in the telogen (resting/shedding) phase. When you understand these phases, you can better support hair through each stage and recognize when something is abnormal versus normal shedding.

The total hair growth cycle lasts 2-7 years depending on genetics, age, health, and various other factors. Supporting each phase with proper nutrition, circulation, and hair care significantly impacts overall hair health and growth rate.

Key Concept

The hair growth cycle consists of three phases: anagen (active growth lasting 2-7 years), catagen (transition lasting 2-3 weeks), and telogen (resting lasting 3 months). Understanding where your hair is in the cycle helps explain normal shedding and optimize care.

The Three Hair Growth Phases Explained

1

Anagen Phase (Growth Phase)

This is the active growth phase where hair is actively being produced by the follicle. The anagen phase lasts 2-7 years (varying by genetics, with women typically having longer anagen phases than men). During this phase, hair grows approximately 0.3-0.4 millimeters per day, or about 4-7 inches per year. The longer your anagen phase, the longer your hair can potentially grow. Hair during anagen is firmly attached to the follicle and has a white bulb at the root. About 85-90% of scalp hair is in anagen at any given time. Supporting anagen with proper nutrition, circulation, and stress management is critical.

2

Catagen Phase (Transition Phase)

This brief transition phase lasts only 2-3 weeks. During catagen, the hair follicle stops actively producing hair and begins to shrink. The hair bulb detaches from the dermal papilla (the nutrient source), and the follicle begins moving upward toward the skin surface. Hair in catagen is no longer receiving active nourishment. Only 1-3% of scalp hair is in catagen at any time. This is the shortest phase but a critical transition point. At the end of catagen, the follicle enters the resting phase.

3

Telogen Phase (Resting/Shedding Phase)

The telogen phase lasts approximately 3-4 months. During this time, the hair is no longer connected to the nutrient source and remains in the follicle in a resting state. The hair gradually moves further up the follicle toward the skin surface. At the end of telogen, the hair is shed naturally, and a new anagen hair begins growing from the same follicle. This is normal, healthy shedding. You typically shed 50-150 hairs per day, and losing hair with a white bulb at the root indicates telogen shedding (which is normal and healthy). About 10-15% of scalp hair is in telogen at any time.

Factors That Affect Hair Growth Cycles

  • Genetics: Your anagen phase length is primarily determined by genetics. This controls maximum hair length potential.
  • Age: Anagen phase shortens with age, which is why older individuals may experience shorter hair maximum length and increased shedding.
  • Nutrition: Protein, iron, zinc, and B vitamins directly support anagen phase duration and hair growth rate.
  • Hormones: Hormonal changes (pregnancy, postpartum, menopause) can shift hair from anagen to telogen prematurely, causing temporary hair loss.
  • Stress: Chronic stress can push hair into telogen phase prematurely, causing telogen effluvium (temporary shedding).
  • Health conditions: Thyroid issues, autoimmune conditions, and nutritional deficiencies affect cycle timing.
  • Medications: Some medications can alter the hair growth cycle.

How to Optimize Each Phase

Support Anagen Phase:

  • Consume adequate protein (hair is 95% protein)
  • Ensure sufficient iron and zinc intake
  • Maintain scalp circulation through massage
  • Manage stress through meditation or exercise
  • Get adequate sleep (growth hormone peaks during sleep)

Support Catagen & Telogen Phases:

  • Minimize hair damage to prevent premature shedding
  • Be gentle during styling to avoid mechanical stress
  • Use moisturizing treatments as hair loses access to nutrients
  • Avoid harsh chemicals that may stress follicles

Normal vs. Abnormal Shedding

Is Your Shedding Normal?

Normal shedding: 50-150 hairs per day, hairs have a white bulb at the root, shedding consistent day-to-day. This represents telogen shedding and is completely healthy.

Abnormal shedding (consult a dermatologist): More than 150 hairs per day consistently, significant increase from baseline, hair loss from specific areas, hairs without bulbs, or accompanied by scalp symptoms.

Related Habits

Understanding your hair growth cycle helps you implement other habits more effectively. Support each phase through scalp massage for circulation, proper nutrition for anagen support, and protective styling to minimize damage during vulnerable telogen phase.
